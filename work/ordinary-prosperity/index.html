How does an ordinary person gain economic choice?

Ordinary Prosperity Research studies how people who rely mainly on labor income—and do not begin with extraordinary inheritance or entrepreneurial windfalls—can build stability, resilience, autonomy, and wider life options over time.

Core mechanism

Prosperity is a chain, not a single balance-sheet number.

01Labor and human capital

Skills, health, institutions, and labor-market access shape earning capacity.

02Labor income

Income level, stability, bargaining power, and taxes determine available resources.

03Disposable surplus

Consumption, housing, family obligations, and public services determine what remains.

04Asset ownership

Savings become claims on housing, businesses, markets, pensions, or other productive assets.

05Risk absorption

Liquidity, insurance, social protection, and diversified assets reduce fragility.

06Economic optionality

Resilience becomes the ability to refuse, move, retrain, care, retire, or pursue long-horizon goals.

Research design

Separate mechanisms, institutions, life stages, and evidence.

Mechanisms

Human capital, surplus formation, asset ownership, housing, family, social insurance, and resilience form the main research axes.

Institutional contexts

Country studies show how taxation, housing systems, education, labor markets, welfare, and pensions change the same underlying choices.

Life course

Labor-market entry, family formation, housing, accumulation, shocks, and retirement are treated as distinct transitions.

Evidence discipline

Income, wealth, consumption, security, and optionality remain distinct; averages, medians, distributions, stocks, flows, and causal claims are not collapsed together.
